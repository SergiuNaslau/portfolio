Situation · Introduction
Five departments.
One process.

The internal portal should allow for a better way of processing the information required from different personas — providing assurances or other details. Essentially, the goal was to collapse a 5-step process done by different departments, either online or offline, into one unified experience.

The client needed a solution that would help different users obtain medical support on different medical situations and products. This solution comes under the form of a wizard form that filters down to the smallest detail — serving multiple devices and user roles from one coherent flow.

"Five steps. Different departments. Online and offline. Collapsed into one."
5→1
Departments and steps consolidated into a single wizard flow across all devices
Before · Fragmented
5 departments, manual handoffs
  • Separate online and offline steps with no single source of truth
  • Users passed between departments — context lost at each handoff
  • Claim completion rate unknown; no structured flow to measure
  • Device support limited — no mobile or tablet optimisation
→
After · Unified wizard
One flow, three touchpoints
  • Single wizard handles all departments in sequence — no handoffs
  • Context built progressively; micro-level detail available per step
  • Completion rate above 50% — measurable and improving
  • Fully responsive: desktop, tablet, and mobile all tested
Task · Problem Space
Rebuild the wizard for
a mature experience

As UX designer, the opportunity was to rebuild the wizard so that a mature experience could be created — providing support on different touchpoints: desktop, tablet, mobile. Using the current strict design system, a solution had to be built that aligns with their requirements.

L1
Consider the environments
Layer 1 took in consideration the environments — ensuring the experience felt native and consistent across desktop, tablet, and mobile. Each touchpoint tested independently to confirm parity, not just responsiveness.
L2
Focus on actual content and build visibility
Layer 2 focused on the actual content and building visibility for the user when asking "where am I?" — at every moment in the wizard, users knew their current step, what was completed, and what came next. No more getting lost mid-form.
L3
Tie everything into a mature experience
Layer 3 tied everything together into a mature experience — an introductory page and a setup page at the start; micro-level information on step 2 to answer promptly to the user's needs. Basically: first page sets context, second page drills in.
Action · Design & Solution
Context first.
Detail second.

The new design follows the rule of one master layout that could power multiple patient-based wizard steps. On the first page, context is set. On the second (or the rest), micro-level information is added to answer the user's needs promptly and accurately. We know sharing feedback or taking a moment on that step is the right call — so we designed the tool to make it easy.

UX & usability impact

  • 50% reduction in claim submission time — users moved faster through the wizard
  • 40% lower error rate — progressive disclosure reduced decision fatigue
  • Users no longer got "lost" mid-form — step visibility resolved orientation completely
  • Context carried between steps — zero re-entry of information across the flow

Business & operational impact

  • Completion rate above 50% — measurable for the first time due to structured flow
  • 80% of users rated the wizard easy to use on all 3 touch-points in closed testing
  • Five-department handoff eliminated as a source of errors and delay
  • Platform ready for future medical product extensions using the same master layout
Reflections
What this project taught
Structure reduces anxiety
In high-stakes contexts like medical claims, knowing "where am I?" isn't a nice-to-have — it's the difference between completion and abandonment. Step visibility was the single highest-impact change.
One master layout, many contexts
Designing a single flexible layout that could power multiple wizard steps — rather than unique screens per step — cut design inconsistency and made the experience feel coherent across all five former departments.
Multi-device isn't a feature — it's a baseline
Medical staff don't sit at desks. Designing for tablet and mobile first — rather than retrofitting — meant the wizard worked naturally in the environments where it would actually be used.
