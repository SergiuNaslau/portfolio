Situation · Introduction
The perfect bridge between
freedom and flexibility.

The research module is the bridge that brings together the Unity VR environment with the content management system. It provides the freedom and flexibility that clients never had — being able to create, customise, and research their own VR environments.

The current VR assessment helps brands study audience online groups. This flexibility allows 20% more growth from a well-structured group of stakeholders that know that they come together can build a jump in several items. As time passes, the worries and needs of the stakeholders grow.

Task · Problem Space
Friction at every
step of the process

When set out to discuss some of the frustration points, the research focused on the following directions — each uncovered through stakeholder sessions with clients, analysts, and developers.

The client depends heavily on the developers — they cannot perform direct tasks from the user's interface, relying on third-party software to do so
Doing A/B testing was almost impossible without adding extra wait costs and developer hours
The customer needed simplified onboarding that shows them what they are doing and what needs to be validated
Users were easily lost in the tool — not controlling, not being told what their next step was in their project. Causing a "figures fetched" confusion state
Problem Statement
"How might we enable clients to measure and customise the VR shopping experience both internally and externally — carrying a seamless A/B testing process and providing the freedom to customise environments to meet their specific needs?"
Root cause identified
The tool had a user-of-onboarding that would show clients information about their project data — but not allow them to understand the data in their project. The team called this "figures fetched": teams had the data but lacked the lens to interpret it.
Action · Deliverables & Process
Designed with all
parties at the table

After engaging in discussions with key stakeholders including the product manager, product owner, business analyst, and developer team, a carefully designed set-up flow was crafted. This carefully designed flow ensures maximum value for all parties involved.

Output 01
Stakeholder alignment sessions
Incorporating valuable feedback and aligning with current issue boards. Cross-functional collaboration across multiple functions — each team highlighting crucial insights that have since been thoughtfully considered in shaping the ultimate outcome.
Output 02
User onboarding redesign
A new onboarding was designed that would show clients not just what data exists, but what it means in the context of their project. Users would see their last modification, current status, and what needs validation — eliminating the "figures fetched" confusion state.
Output 03
Phased delivery approach
To manage competing stakeholder needs without derailing delivery, resources were split into three phases — balancing independence for clients, information for the team, and support for client needs independently.
Output 04
Final interface design
The collaborative nature of the process — with multiple functions involved — meant that every step highlighting crucial insights was thoughtfully considered in shaping the final design. Validated and iterated with real users before release.

Reflections
What this project taught
Data without context is noise
The "figures fetched" problem was a UX failure dressed as a data problem. Users had all the information — they just couldn't interpret it. Adding meaning to data is as much a design challenge as building the feature itself.
Stakeholder alignment is research
Cross-functional sessions with PMs, owners, analysts, and developers weren't just process — they were research. Each function held a piece of the problem. The final design was better because all voices shaped it before a single screen was drawn.
Phase resources like a product
Splitting the 80/40/20 resource allocation across phases wasn't arbitrary — it was a design decision about what mattered most. Treating resource distribution as a design artefact brought clarity to the whole team on priorities.
